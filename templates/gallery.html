...Some Examples of Data Analysis...

I analyzed many things, including the distributions of responses in terms of time collected, investment types (all, self-directed only, and direct investment only), gender, age, ESG awareness, years of work experience, education level, seniority at work, industry of work, annual gross income, financial information souces, and current investment activness. I also plotted the training errors to find the best K and see the performance of predicting different letters in R, F, T, and M.

I also analyzed the relationship between different investment types and some demographic features to visualize any trends or correlations.


As shown below, as I started to find trends in which platform had the highest yield of responses, and focused my efforts there, more responses can flooding in with time.

K=5 seemed to have the smallest error, so that is the K I used for the final model.

Moreover, compartmentalizing the error for each letter, it seems that predictions for R is the best: although the error rate is still high at about 20%, it is generally lower than that of the other attributes.


I found that the Chi-Squared Test between different genders and investment types is 0.00026, which is less than 0.05, meaning that there is a significant correlation! During data exploration and model construction, this boosted my confidence in the performance of the model, as at least gender seems to be a significant predictor.


As one can see from below, most respondents who chose the 'Self-directed' investment option is in the education industry, whereas those who haven't started working mostly chose to consult Asset Managers, which shows a lower risk appetite. This makes sense, because they have less experience and inital capital to invest.


Also, most respondents tend to have at least some consideration for ESG implications of their investment, as shown, a total of 67% of respondents chose "Very Much", "A Good Deal", or "Somewhat" for ESG considerations of their investments.

In addition, my analysis showed that most respondents had relatively little investment experience, with most having currently invested <10% of their net worth.
